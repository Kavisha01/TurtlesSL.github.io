Threats To Turtles

Why Are Sea Turtles Endangered?



  • Along the southeast coast of the United States, hundreds of hatchling turtles leave their nests each year and swim into the Atlantic Ocean. Sadly, just one in 1,000 to 10,000 people will reach maturity. Young and adult sea turtles confront incredible natural challenges, but it is the growing risks brought on by humans that are putting them in danger of going extinct. Except for the loggerhead, which is designated as threatened, all sea turtles found in American waters are now federally listed as endangered.
  • Worldwide, six of the seven sea turtle species are classified as threatened or endangered due to human actions and lifestyles. From getting caught in fishing gear to people eating their meat and eggs, there are lots of ways that these animals are affected by people.


  • Entanglement in fishing gear :
    become entangled in fishing equipment and suffer injuries from bomb fishing, shrimp nets, and longlines. .
  • Coastal development :
    Constructing on mangroves, nesting beaches, and other significant coastal regions can influence turtles' capacity to nest and feed, as well as their ability to produce hatchlings.
  • Consumption and illicit trading of turtle eggs and meat :
    Despite both being prohibited in the majority of nations, green turtle meat is still consumed and its eggs are widely believed to have aphrodisiac properties.
  • Plastic and other marine debris :
    Plastic and other marine garbage can harm nests and hatchlings, and turtles ingest it, get tangled in it, and must navigate through it in the ocean and on beaches.
  • Global Warming :
    Rising temperatures are warming the sand, which can only lead to the birth of females. Sea level rise, which can erode nesting beaches and result in coral bleaching, another effect of climate change, also affects hawksbills' critical habitat.


By fishing

Each year, thousands to tens of thousands of sea turtles are thought to perish as a result of fishing. Turtles collected in gill nets, trawls, and long lines are released as bycatch. Even those that manage to avoid fishing nets endure harm because their food supply and environment are disturbed.



Egg and Meat Consumption and Illegal Trade

Turtle meat, primarily from green turtles, is still consumed despite the fact that many nations consider turtle eggs to be pesticides (despite there being no scientific foundation for this claim)



Plastic and other marine debris

Turtles consume plastic, become caught in it, pass through it while swimming and walking on beaches, and microplastics can harm nests and young.



What can we do to save Sri Lanka's sea turtles?

  • Eliminate marine trash that could entangle or unintentionally ingest sea turtles.
  • Help preserve our beaches and ocean clean by taking part in coastal cleanups and reducing your use of plastic. Sea turtles and other marine life could be harmed by trash in the water.
  • Bring your own shopping bags and reusable water bottles. Avoid releasing balloons into the air because they will probably wind up in the water where sea turtles may mistake them for prey and eat them.
  • Preserve sea turtle safety and darkness on nesting beaches. Turn off, cover, or reroute any lights that can be seen from the shore. Sea turtle hatchlings are confused by lights, and females that are nesting are deterred from coming to the shore to lay their eggs.
  • Keep nesting turtles, nests, and hatchlings undisturbed. Participate in organized sea turtle watch programs that understand how to monitor breeding sea turtles safely.
  • In order to prevent sea turtles from being turned away, remove recreational beach equipment including chairs, umbrellas, and boats at night.
  • Before you leave the beach, patch up any holes and destroy any sandcastles. They could become a barrier for hatchlings or turtles that are building nests.
